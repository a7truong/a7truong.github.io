List of Classes

Computer Science

CS 115/CS 116 (Introduction to Computer Science 1/2) Introduction to fundamentals of programming through a functioanl style. Concepts of programming techniques in designing, testing and documentation. [Language: Scheme and Python]

CS 136 (Elementary Algorithm Design and Data Abstraction) Transition to imperative programming techniques from a functional style point of view. Introduction to algorithm design, algorithm analysis, and information management. Data structures discussed and implemented include lists, stacks, queues, and trees. [Language: C]

CS 240 (Data Structures and Data Management) Introduction to effective methods of data organization, focusing on data structures, their algorithms, and the performance. Topics include priority queues, sorting, dictionaries, data structures for text processing.

CS 241 (Foundations of Sequential Programs) The relationship between high-level languages and the computer architecture that underlies the implementation, including basic machine architecture, assemblers, specification and translation of programming languages, linkers and loaders, block-structured languages, parameter passing mechanisms, and comparison of programming languages. [Language: C++]

CS 245 (Logic and Computation) Introduction to propositional and predicate logic. Algorithm analysis including many theorems and implications such as soundness and completeness, unproveability, undecidability and correctness proofs for program constructions.

CS 246 (Object-Oriented Software Development) Introduction to object-oriented programming and techniques for software development. Implementation of design patterns and medium-sized programs while selecting appropriate data structure. Writing reusable code and reusing existing code; basic performance issues; debug and test suites. [Language: C++]

CS 251 (Computer Organization and Design) Overview of computer organization and performance. Basics of digital logic design. Combinational and sequential elements. Data representation and manipulation. Basics of processor design, pipelining, memory hierarchies, and multiprocessors.

CS 341 (Algorithms) Study of efficient algorithms and effective algorithm designing techniques. Program design with emphasis on program efficiency. Topics include divide and conquer algorithms, recurrences, greedy algorithms, dynamic programming, graph search and backtrack, problems without algorithms, and NP-completeness. [Language: C++]

CS 348 (Introduction to Database Management) Introduction to fundamentals of database technology from three viewpoints: those of the database user, the database designer, and the database administrator. Introduction to database management systems (DBMS); focusing on its functionality and its interfaces. Topics include: introduction to database systems, relational database systems, database design methodology, SQL and interfaces, database application development, concept of transactions, JDBC, schema analysis, transaction management, and security. [Language: SQL and Java]

Mathematics / Statistics

MATH 135 (Algebra for Honours Mathematics) An introduction to the language of mathematics and proof techniques through a study of the basic algebraic systems of mathematics: the integers, the integers modulo n, the rational numbers, the real numbers, the complex numbers and polynomials.

MATH 136 (Linear Algebra 1 for Honours Mathematics) Systems of linear equations, matrix algebra, elementary matrices, computational issues. Real n-space, vector spaces and subspaces, basis and dimension, rank of a matrix, linear transformations and matrix representations. Determinants, eigenvalues and diagonalization, applications.

MATH 137 (Calculus 1 for Honours Mathematics) Rational, trigonometric, exponential, and power functions of a real variable; composites and inverses. Absolute values and inequalities. Limits and continuity. Derivatives and the linear approximation. Applications of the derivative, including curve sketching, optimization, related rates, and Newton's method. The Mean Value Theorem and error bounds. Introduction to the Riemann Integral and approximations. Anti-derivatives and the Fundamental Theorem. Change of variable, areas and rate integrals.

MATH 138 (Calculus 2 for Honours Mathematics) Methods of integration. Further applications of the integral. Improper integrals. Linear and separable differential equations and applications. Vector (parametric) curves in R2. Convergence of sequences and series. Tests for convergence. Functions defined as power series. Taylor polynomials, Taylor's Theorem, Taylor series and polynomial approximation.

MATH 239 (Introduction to Combinatorics) Introduction to graph theory: colourings, matchings, connectivity, planarity. Introduction to combinatorial analysis: generating series, recurrence relations, binary strings, plane trees.

STAT 230 (Probability) The laws of probability, discrete and continuous random variables, expectation, central limit theorem.

STAT 231 (Statistics) Empirical problem solving, measurement systems, causal relationships, statistical models, estimation, confidence intervals, tests of significance.

STAT 373 (Regression and Forecasting Methods in Finance) Application of regression and time series models in finance; multiple regression; algebraic and geometric representation of least squares; inference methods - confidence intervals and hypothesis tests, ANOVA, prediction; model building and assessment; time series modeling; autoregressive AR(1) models - fitting, assessment and prediction; moving average smoothing, seasonal adjustment; non-stationarity and differencing.

Accounting and Financial Management / Arts

AFM 101 (Introduction to Financial Accounting) Introduction to financial accounting. The preparation and use of financial statements is examined. The accounting cycle, assets and liabilities reporting.

AFM 102 (Introduction to Managerial Accounting) Introduction to the preparation and use of accounting information for management decision-making and reporting. Cost behaviour, cost accumulation systems and short and long-term decision models.

AFM 121 (Introduction to Global Financial Markets) Describes the role of finance in the modern global economy and introduces some basic principles of financial decision-making.

AFM 131 (Introduction to Business in North America) The functional areas of business: finance, personnel administration, production, marketing, and accounting are examined within differing organizational structures. Coverage also includes study of the principles of effective management and the financial system as a source of corporate capital.

AFM 272/AFM 372 (Corporate Finance 1/2) Corporate financial focusing on decision-making. Topics include time value of money, capital budgeting, cost of capital, security issuance, capital structure, payout policy and dividends, short term finance, and risk management. Where suitable, topics are treated from a mathematical and quantitative perspective.

AFM 425 (Fixed Income Securities) Introduction to various aspects of fixed income investments, including valuation, risk management, portfolio management, interest rate models, interest rate derivatives, and institutional features of bond markets.

ECON 101 (Introduction to Microeconomics) Introduction to microeconomic analysis relevant for understanding the Canadian economy. The behaviour of individual consumers and producers, the determination of market prices for commodities and resources, and the role of government policy in the functioning of the market system are the main topics covered.

ECON 102 (Introduction to Macroeconomics) Introduction to macroeconomic analysis relevant for understanding the Canadian economy as a whole. The determinants of national output, the unemployment rate, the price level (inflation), interest rates, the money supply and the balance of payments, and the role of government fiscal and monetary policy are the main topics covered.

ECON 202 (Macroeconomic Theory 1) Theory of the determination of income/output (GDP), employment, unemployment, prices (inflation), and interest rates; an analysis of monetary and fiscal policy.

ECON 231 (Introduction to International Economics) Theory of comparative advantage and the gains from trade; tariff theory; concepts and measurement of balance of payments; exchange rate systems; reform of international monetary system.

ENGL 119 (Communications in Mathematics & Computer Science) Aims to build students' oral and written communication skills to prepare them for academic and workplace demands. Working independently and in collaboration with others, students will analyze and produce various written and spoken forms of communication. Projects and assignments based on materials from Mathematics and Computer Science.

CO-OP / Professional Development

PD 1 (Co-op Fundamentals) Provides resources and activitiess with the tools to be competitive in employment and preparation in work place success.

PD 2 (Critical Reflection and Reporting Writing) Theories and concepts underlying critical reflection, alternative approaches to critical reflection during a work term, alternative ways to report on critical thinking, and best practices related to organization and writing of work term reports. Develop analytical, critical thinking, and report writing skills by focusing on critical reflection and thinking, analysis, and best practices in report writing.

PD 3 (Communication) Understand the importance and function of communication, particularly communication in the workplace, with a view to assessing, monitoring, and improving their own communications skills. The course focuses on workplace communication, including emails, letters, and voicemail; conversation and meeting dynamics; oral presentations; ethics; and personal style. The focus will always be on communication quality rather than on the formal conventions of these communication genres.

PD 4 (Teamwork) Introduction to the theory and research on teamwork and small group communication and relates this material to students’ own experiences in teams. The course’s primary objective is to teach students to think critically about how they and others behave in teams.

PD 5 (Project Management) Introduction to basic project management techniques and practices such as planning, proposing, scheduling, budgeting, supervising, delegating and setting priorities. Upon completion, students will understand how these techniques and practices are used in an organizational context, as well as the human issues connected to the application of such practices.
